# Curses! Landing Page — Promotional Narrative Framework

> **Purpose:** This document defines the voice, story arc, messaging pillars, competitive framing, delight moments, and CTA strategy for the Curses! public-facing marketing landing page. It is a strategic brief for the copywriting agent to execute against. Everything here is direction, not final copy.

---

## 1. Brand Voice Guidelines

The voice of the Curses! landing page should feel like **a talented friend pulling you aside at a party to tell you about the coolest thing they've been working on.** There's reverence for the craft but zero stuffiness. Think: the confidence of someone who knows exactly what they built and why it matters, delivered with the warmth of someone who genuinely wants you to have a great time at the table.

### Register

Conversational-elevated. The vocabulary should skew smart but never academic. We can use the language of fantasy and storytelling ("forge your character," "the world awaits," "your story begins here") but it should always feel *earned* rather than decorative. We talk like creators because we are creators. We talk like players because we are players.

### Personality Traits

**Confident without arrogance.** We know Curses! is the best Daggerheart toolkit available. We say so plainly. We let the features speak and we frame them with clarity, not with hedging language. "The most complete Daggerheart platform ever built" is a statement of fact when you look at the feature set. We never need to diminish competitors to make our case.

**Warm without being saccharine.** We're inviting, not begging. The page should make someone feel like they've discovered something special, like they're being let in on a secret that's about to blow up. There's an undercurrent of "you're going to love this" that respects the reader's intelligence.

**Fantasy-infused without being corny.** The app lives in a world of curses, domains, and eldritch forces. The copy can breathe that air. But it should feel like a *vibe*, not a Renaissance fair barker. The fantasy language is atmospheric texture, not the load-bearing structure of the messaging. When we describe features, we describe what they actually do. When we set mood, we let the world seep in.

**Creator-forward and community-rooted.** This platform was built by actual play creators, for actual play creators (and their players). That pedigree matters. We name the cast. We reference the stream. We treat the indie audio-fiction community as the culture we belong to, because we do. The landing page should feel like it came from *within* that world, not from a company marketing *to* it.

### Tone Palette

| Context | Tone |
|---|---|
| Hero section | Bold, atmospheric, slightly mysterious |
| Feature blocks | Direct, confident, benefit-forward |
| Streaming/creator section | Energetic, insider, community-proud |
| Pricing section | Transparent, generous, no-nonsense |
| New player section | Encouraging, safe, judgment-free |
| CTAs | Action-oriented, inviting, low-friction |

### Words We Love

forge, craft, wield, build, your story, at the table, in the moment, live, real-time, purpose-built, Daggerheart-native, complete toolkit, from the ground up, streamlined

### Words We Avoid

"revolutionary" (overused), "just" (minimizing), "simple" (condescending when describing a robust tool), "unique" without evidence, corporate jargon ("leverage," "synergize," "solution"), anything that sounds like a SaaS landing page selling project management software

## 2. Narrative Arc — The Emotional Journey

The page should read like a story with rising action. A visitor arrives curious (or skeptical) and leaves either signing up or bookmarking the page to come back. Every section escalates the desire to participate.

### Section 1: The Hook (Hero)

**Emotional beat: Awe + intrigue**

This is a full-bleed, atmospheric moment. Dark background. The Curses! logo. A single bold statement that communicates what this is and *why it matters* in one breath. The hero should make someone feel like they've walked into a world. The aesthetic here borrows from Griflan's bold typography and editorial restraint, but the content is unmistakably fantasy. Minimal text, maximum presence.

The hero answers one question: "What am I looking at and why should I care?" The answer is something like: this is the definitive Daggerheart platform, and a show you need to watch. One sentence. Then a sub-line that grounds it: character building, campaign management, streaming tools. Then the primary CTA.

The background could incorporate the existing `fantasy-monster-bg.webp` or `char-sheet-public-bg.webp` assets with a deep parallax or subtle ken-burns animation, overlaid with the dark radial gradient the app already uses (`radial-gradient` on `#0a100d`).

### Section 2: The Promise (Value Proposition Overview)

**Emotional beat: Recognition + excitement**

Four compact, scannable value props with icons or small illustrations. This is the "what you get at a glance" moment. Each prop is a single headline + one supporting sentence. The layout borrows from D&D Beyond's tabbed feature blocks, but stripped of carousel noise. Clean grid. Bold type. Lots of breathing room.

The four pillars: Built for Streamers → Free for Players → Campaign Tools → New Player Friendly. Each links down to its expanded section below, creating a navigation anchor system.

### Section 3: The Showcase (Feature Deep-Dives)

**Emotional beat: Desire + "I need this"**

This is the meat. Each of the four selling points gets a full section with a headline, 2-4 sentences of supporting copy, and a visual (screenshot, animation, or illustrated diagram). These sections alternate visual alignment (image-left/text-right, then swap) for visual rhythm, à la Griflan's case study layouts.

The order is deliberate:

1. **Streaming & Creator Tools** comes first because it's the most differentiating. Nobody else has this.
2. **Campaign Management** comes second because it's the deepest feature set and validates the "complete toolkit" claim.
3. **Free for Players** comes third because by now the visitor is interested and the pricing removes the last barrier.
4. **New Player Onboarding** comes last because it broadens the audience. Even if *you* don't need handholding, you know someone who does, and now you can invite them.

### Section 4: The World (Curses! Stream + Show)

**Emotional beat: Community + FOMO**

This section introduces the Curses! actual play as the *living proof* of everything described above. The cast is named and celebrated. The stream schedule is prominent. The tone shifts from "look what this tool can do" to "look what this tool enables." This is where the page becomes warm and human. Photos or stylized portraits of the cast. The Twitch embed or a link to the latest VOD. Wednesday 8:30 PM ET in big, unmissable type.

This section also serves as the bridge to the podcast announcement (August 2026 on Fable & Folly). A "Coming Soon" beat that builds anticipation without over-promising.

### Section 5: The Proof (Social Proof / Credibility)

**Emotional beat: Trust + validation**

This could be quotes from the cast, community member testimonials, viewer count milestones, or notable mentions. If hard metrics aren't available yet, this section can instead spotlight the creator pedigrees (What's in The Rift, Quest Friends, REDACTED, Dungeons and Drimbus, Tales of the Ever After) as implicit endorsements. The message: these people chose to build and play here. That means something.

### Section 6: The Invitation (Pricing + CTA)

**Emotional beat: Generosity + urgency**

Clean, two-column pricing layout. Player column: FREE, forever, no catch, full character builder, join campaigns, roll dice. GM column: $5/month, everything a player gets plus campaigns, encounters, command center, session logs, adversary catalog, homebrew workshop, streaming overlays. The framing should emphasize what $5 *replaces* (the value of consolidating multiple subscriptions and tools into one platform).

Primary CTA here is "Create Your First Character" (free) with a secondary "Start Your Campaign" (GM tier). The pricing section should feel generous, not transactional. We're proud of the free tier, and the paid tier is priced to be an obvious yes.

### Section 7: The Close (Final CTA + Footer)

**Emotional beat: Momentum + belonging**

A single, bold closing line. A final CTA. Social links (Twitch, Bluesky, Instagram, YouTube, TikTok). The Man in Jumpsuit logo. Discord community link. The feeling should be: you've seen what this is, now come be part of it.

## 3. Key Messaging Pillars

### Pillar 1: Built for Streamers and Podcasters

**Headline concepts:**

- "Your Stream Deserves Better Than a Shared Google Sheet"
- "The First Daggerheart Platform Built for the Camera"
- "Stream-Ready from Day One"

**Messaging direction:**

Every other TTRPG tool treats streaming as an afterthought, if they think about it at all. Curses! was designed from the ground up by people who stream. The Twitch overlay is a native feature, sized and styled for OBS browser sources at 380×220px with transparent backgrounds. Character stats refresh live on-stream every 90 seconds. HP drops, Hope spends, condition changes — your audience sees them the moment they happen.

The OBS dice overlay renders full 3D dice animations with campaign-synced WebSocket connections, critical-hit goldenrod glows, and automatic fade-out timing so the overlay lives cleanly in your scene without manual intervention. There's a separate dice log overlay for persistent roll history. Your character sheets have public share URLs with expiring tokens so viewers can browse the full sheet without compromising account security.

The Command Center gives GMs a heads-up display of every player's vitals — HP percentage, stress levels, armor slots, Hope count, active conditions — all color-coded by danger state and sorted by who's hurting most. It's a mission control dashboard designed to be visible on a second monitor while you narrate. These aren't features bolted onto a character sheet app. This *is* the streaming toolkit, and it also happens to be a phenomenal character builder.

### Pillar 2: Free for Players, $5/Month for GMs

**Headline concepts:**

- "Players Play Free. Full Stop."
- "Five Dollars Replaces Five Subscriptions"
- "The Complete GM Toolkit for Less Than a Latte"

**Messaging direction:**

Character creation, character management, campaign participation, dice rolling — all of it is free for players, forever. No trial periods, no feature-gating, no "free tier" that feels like a demo. Players get the full character builder with guided creation, trait assignment, domain card selection, equipment loadout, leveling, and the complete SRD reference. They can join campaigns, view their party, roll dice, and manage their sheets without spending a cent.

For GMs, $5/month unlocks the entire campaign management layer: unlimited campaigns, encounter designer console with environment systems and adversary catalog, session logging, invite management, the Command Center party dashboard, streaming overlays, the homebrew workshop for creating custom ancestries, classes, weapons, armor, loot, domain cards, and communities. Consider what that replaces: a VTT subscription, a separate encounter builder, a homebrew hosting tool, a session tracker, and a streaming overlay service. Five dollars. One platform. Everything you need.

The pricing model exists because we believe the barrier to playing Daggerheart should be zero, and the barrier to *running* Daggerheart should be nearly zero. GMs already do the most work at the table. The least we can do is make their digital toolkit affordable.

### Pillar 3: Campaign Tools That Outclass the Competition

**Headline concepts:**

- "The GM Toolkit That Doesn't Exist Anywhere Else"
- "Everything Between Session Zero and the Final Boss"
- "Campaign Management, Actually Designed"

**Messaging direction:**

Most Daggerheart tools stop at the character sheet. Curses! keeps going. The campaign system supports full party management with member rosters, invite links for easy onboarding, and real-time WebSocket connections so GMs and players stay synchronized. The encounter designer isn't a simple initiative tracker — it's a living console where you stage adversaries from the built-in catalog, load environmental hazards with activatable features, track HP and conditions per adversary, and log rolls as they happen.

The adversary catalog is filterable by type, tier, and difficulty, with full stat blocks rendered inline. The environment system lets you load ambient features into encounters — things that affect the battlefield, cost Fear to activate, or require player rolls to resist. The session panel tracks scheduling and notes. The homebrew workshop provides structured creation forms for every content type in the system, with live markdown preview and source-badge differentiation so players always know what's official SRD and what's custom.

And the whole thing is connected by WebSocket — real-time dice broadcasts, GM-to-player pings that scroll to specific elements on a player's sheet, forced critical rolls for dramatic moments, roll requests that stage directly into a player's dice panel. This is a campaign management system that understands how modern TTRPG sessions actually work, especially sessions played for an audience.

### Pillar 4: Perfect for First-Time Players

**Headline concepts:**

- "Your Friend's First Character, Built in Ten Minutes"
- "No Rulebook Required (Seriously)"
- "The Gentlest On-Ramp in Tabletop"

**Messaging direction:**

The character builder walks new players through every decision in order: class, subclass, ancestry (including mixed ancestry support), community, trait assignment, weapons, armor, starting equipment, domain cards, review. Each step explains what it means and why it matters. SRD descriptions are collapsible — available when you want the detail, tucked away when you don't. Source badges distinguish official content from homebrew so new players never accidentally pick something their GM hasn't approved.

But the real magic is what happens during play. The GM's ping system lets them tap any element on a player's character sheet — a stat, a feature, a domain card — and the player's screen scrolls to it with a highlight animation. When the GM says "roll Agility," the player doesn't need to know where Agility lives. The GM sends a roll request that pre-populates the player's dice roller with the correct dice pool, and they just hit "Roll." Domain card descriptions, class feature effects, condition definitions — everything a player needs to understand their character is right there on the sheet, explained in context. The entire rules reference is searchable and browsable without leaving the app.

This means a GM can invite a brand-new player, hand them a join link, help them build a character in minutes, and then guide them through an entire session without that player ever needing to open a PDF. That's the promise: the barrier to entry for Daggerheart is now zero.

## 4. Differentiator Framing

### vs. D&D Beyond

D&D Beyond is the gold standard for *D&D* digital tooling. It's also owned by Hasbro and built entirely around the D&D ecosystem. For Daggerheart players, it doesn't exist. Our positioning here isn't adversarial — it's categorical. We occupy a different space. But we can borrow the *expectation* D&D Beyond has set: players now expect a polished, integrated digital companion for their game. Curses! meets that expectation for Daggerheart.

The framing: "D&D players have had a complete digital toolkit for years. Now Daggerheart players do too."

### vs. Demiplane

Demiplane is the most direct competitor in concept — they're building digital toolkits for non-D&D systems, including a Daggerheart offering. The differentiation is depth and origin. Demiplane is a platform company building tools for many systems. Curses! is a Daggerheart-native platform built by people who run a Daggerheart actual play. Every design decision, every feature priority, every UX choice was made by someone who uses this tool at their own table, on their own stream, every Wednesday night.

The framing: "Built by Daggerheart GMs, for Daggerheart GMs. This isn't a generic toolkit with a Daggerheart skin."

### vs. Alchemy VTT

Alchemy is a virtual tabletop — maps, tokens, tactical movement. Curses! is a campaign management and character platform. They're complementary more than competitive. But if someone is choosing between investing in Alchemy vs. Curses!, the message is: Alchemy gives you a virtual table. Curses! gives you everything *around* the table — the sheets, the tools, the stream overlays, the encounter design, the homebrew system. For groups that play theater-of-the-mind (which is Daggerheart's default mode), Curses! is the complete package without needing a VTT at all.

The framing: "Daggerheart is theater-of-the-mind. Your toolkit should be too."

### vs. Generic Tools (Google Sheets, Notion, Homebrew PDFs)

This is actually the most common "competitor" — GMs cobbling together spreadsheets, shared docs, and Discord bots. The differentiation is integration and professionalism. Curses! replaces the duct-tape stack with a single, purpose-built platform. For streamers especially, the difference between a Google Sheet and a live-updating Twitch overlay with 3D dice is the difference between amateur hour and a production.

The framing: "You've been building your toolkit from spare parts. Here's the real thing."

### The Unifying Angle

Across all comparisons, the throughline is: **Curses! is the only Daggerheart platform built by Daggerheart creators who use it live, on stream, every week.** No other tool can claim that. The product is validated in public, in real-time, in front of an audience. Every pain point the team discovers on Wednesday night becomes a feature by the following week. That feedback loop is our moat.

## 5. Delight Moments

These are specific micro-interactions and copy moments designed to surprise, charm, and reward attention. They should feel like Easter eggs for people who linger, not gimmicks that distract from the core message.

### 5a. The Dice Roll Hero Interaction

When a visitor first lands on the page, the hero section could include a subtle, ambient 3D dice animation — perhaps two d12s slowly tumbling in the background, rendered with the same engine the app uses for its OBS overlay. On click (or on scroll past a certain point), they resolve with a satisfying physics settle. If the visitor gets a critical (matching values), the hero background pulses with the same goldenrod glow the app uses. A tiny line of text appears: "That's a crit. Good omen." This immediately demonstrates the dice engine's quality and creates a personal, memorable first moment. The tech already exists in the codebase (`DiceRoller`, `DiceOverlayClient`).

### 5b. The Living Character Sheet

Somewhere in the feature showcase, embed an interactive mock character sheet — not a screenshot, but a live, stripped-down version of the actual `CharacterSheet` component rendered with sample data. Visitors can click through the trackers, expand domain cards, toggle conditions. It's a "try before you sign up" moment that lets the product sell itself. The read-only `PublicSheetClient` component already supports unauthenticated viewing via share tokens, so the infrastructure exists.

### 5c. The Command Center Heartbeat

In the campaign tools section, show a simulated Command Center grid with four party members. Their HP bars subtly pulse and shift over time — one character slowly takes damage, another heals, conditions flicker on and off. The visitor watches a simulated session unfold in miniature. It communicates the real-time nature of the tool without requiring explanation. The `CommandCenterCard` component with its danger-state color coding (`healthy → wounded → critical → down`) already handles the visual logic.

### 5d. Scroll-Triggered Cast Reveals

In the Curses! show section, each cast member's name and role could animate in on scroll — a staggered fade-up that feels like credits rolling. Each name links to their respective project (REDACTED, Quest Friends, Dungeons and Drimbus, Tales of the Ever After). On hover, a brief audio snippet or quote could appear. This celebrates the cast and gives the section a cinematic quality that reinforces the "this is a production, not a hobby project" message.

### 5e. The "What's Your Class?" Teaser

At the bottom of the page, near the final CTA, a small interactive widget: "What class calls to you?" Six or eight class icons (using the existing SRD class data). Hover over one and a single-sentence description appears with the subclass options. Click one and the CTA dynamically updates: "Build Your [Class Name] →." It's playful, it's personalized, and it funnels directly into the character builder. The class/subclass data is already loaded from the SRD content engine.

## 6. CTA Strategy

### The Core Challenge

We have two user types with different entry points: **players** (free) and **GMs** ($5/month). The page needs to serve both without creating decision paralysis. The worst outcome is someone thinking "I need to figure out the pricing before I can do anything."

### Solution: Lead with Free, Reveal Paid Naturally

The primary CTA throughout the page should always be the free action: **"Create Your First Character"** or **"Start Building."** This is the universal entry point. Everyone — players and GMs alike — starts by creating a character. The GM tier only matters when you want to create a *campaign*, and by that point you're already inside the app and invested.

### Primary CTA

**"Create Your First Character"** (or a shorter variant: "Start Building")

This appears in the hero, at the end of each feature section, and in the pricing block. It's the one action we want above all others. It's free. It requires only an account creation (email or OAuth). The word "first" implies there will be more. The word "character" grounds it in the fantasy.

### Secondary CTA

**"Watch Curses! Live"** (links to Twitch)

This appears in the show section and optionally in the hero as a quieter link. For visitors who aren't ready to sign up but are intrigued, watching the stream is the next-best conversion. They'll see the tools in action and come back.

### Tertiary CTA

**"Start Your Campaign — $5/month"**

This appears *only* in the pricing section, clearly positioned as the GM tier. It shouldn't compete with the free CTA anywhere else on the page. The pricing section handles the player/GM split with a clean two-column layout so the distinction is obvious.

### CTA Placement Map

| Section | Primary CTA | Secondary CTA |
|---|---|---|
| Hero | Create Your First Character | Watch Curses! Live |
| Feature showcase (each block) | Start Building | — |
| Show / Stream section | Watch Curses! Live | Create Your First Character |
| Pricing | Create Your First Character (Player) | Start Your Campaign (GM) |
| Final close | Create Your First Character | Join the Discord |

### Handling the Split Without Confusion

The key insight: **don't present the split until the pricing section.** Every CTA before that is simply "start for free." The pricing section is where we explain: players stay free forever, GMs unlock campaign tools for $5/month. By the time someone reaches that section, they already understand what the platform does and can self-select. The worst thing we could do is put a "Players: Free / GMs: $5" fork in the hero. That creates cognitive load before we've created desire.

### Micro-Copy on CTAs

The CTA buttons should have micro-copy beneath or beside them that reduces friction:

- Under "Create Your First Character": *Free forever. No credit card required.*
- Under "Start Your Campaign": *$5/month. Cancel anytime.*
- Under "Watch Curses! Live": *Wednesdays at 8:30 PM ET on Twitch*

## Appendix: Visual Direction Notes for Front-End

These notes bridge the narrative framework to the front-end implementation, connecting the Griflan aesthetic to the existing Curses! design system.

**Typography:** The app already has a strong type system — `double-pica` for serif headings, `sofia-pro-narrow` for larger UI text, `mestiza-sans` for body, `jetsam-collection-basilea` for display moments. The landing page should lean *heavily* on `double-pica` and `jetsam-collection-basilea` for headlines, reserving `mestiza-sans` for body paragraphs. The Griflan aesthetic is built on bold, oversized type with generous leading. We should push headline sizes larger than anywhere else in the app.

**Color:** The existing palette is perfect for a landing page. The `#0a100d` background with `radial-gradient` overlays creates depth. Burgundy (`burgundy-700` to `burgundy-900`) for primary accents and borders. Gold (`gold-400` to `gold-600`) for highlights, CTAs, and moments of warmth. Steel blue (`steel-400`, `#577399`) for secondary interactive elements. Parchment (`parchment-400` to `parchment-600`) for body text on dark. Coral (`coral-400`) for homebrew/accent badges.

**Spacing:** Griflan uses dramatic whitespace. The landing page should have significantly more vertical padding between sections than the app's internal pages. Think 120-160px between major sections on desktop. Let the content breathe. The dark background makes generous spacing feel atmospheric rather than empty.

**Animation:** The existing keyframes (`fade-in`, `shimmer`, `pulse-glow-goldenrod`, `coral-pulse`) provide a foundation. The landing page should add scroll-triggered reveal animations (intersection observer driven) with the same `fade-in` curve. Nothing bouncy or springy. Everything should feel like it's emerging from shadow — slow, elegant, purposeful. The `glow-gold` and `glow-burgundy` box shadows can be used for hover states on feature cards.

**Dark mode only.** The landing page has no light mode. The app doesn't support it and the aesthetic depends on the dark palette. This is a feature, not a limitation. Dark backgrounds photograph better, stream better, and feel more immersive for a fantasy product.
